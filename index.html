How to Study Biology

Strategy: Making the Most of These Resources

These worked examples show you how to use this hub effectively for different study situations.

✎ Worked Example — I have an exam on Cell Biology tomorrow. Where do I start?
You have not kept up with your Cell Biology lecture notes and your exam is in 24 hours. You feel overwhelmed and do not know where to start.
1Triage your topics: Open the Cell Biology (Part 1) or (Part 2) guide and scan the table of contents. Identify the 3 to 4 topics your professor emphasized most in lecture. Do not try to learn everything.
2Do flashcards first: Run through the flashcards for your priority topics. This builds vocabulary rapidly. You cannot answer questions about processes you cannot name.
3Take the quiz: After flashcards, take the multiple-choice quiz. Every question you get wrong tells you exactly where your gap is. Focus your reading on those gaps, not on things you already know.
4Write one short answer: Before you sleep, write out one short answer response for a high-priority topic, in your own words, without looking at notes. If you can explain it in writing, you understand it.
✓ Result: In 2 to 3 hours you will have covered your highest-priority material with active recall, which is far more effective than passive re-reading.
✎ Worked Example — I keep failing quizzes even though I re-read my notes. Why?
You spend hours re-reading your textbook and notes, but your quiz scores are not improving. You feel like you understand the material but then blank on tests.
1Recognize the illusion of fluency: Re-reading creates a feeling of familiarity, but familiarity is not the same as being able to retrieve information under pressure. This is one of the most common traps in college biology.
2Switch to active recall: Close your notes. Try to write down everything you remember about a topic on a blank page. Then check what you missed. This retrieval practice strengthens memory far more than re-reading.
3Use these guides differently: Instead of reading the content sections, start with the quiz or short answer. Struggle with the question first, then go read the explanation. This "desirable difficulty" makes learning stick.
✓ Result: Students who switch from passive re-reading to active retrieval practice typically see significant quiz and exam improvement within two to three weeks.

✎ Worked Example — What even is the "central dogma" and why does it matter?
Your Bio I professor keeps saying "central dogma" but you have no idea what it means or why it is such a big deal.
1The basic flow: The central dogma describes how genetic information moves through a cell: DNA → RNA → Protein. DNA is the master copy stored in the nucleus. RNA is a working copy that carries instructions to the ribosomes. Protein does the actual work.
2Why "dogma"? When Francis Crick proposed this in 1958, it was a foundational organizing principle — information flows in one direction. (We now know some exceptions, like retroviruses, but the central dogma still describes the vast majority of biology.)
3Real-world connection: mRNA vaccines (like COVID-19 vaccines) exploit this flow. The vaccine delivers mRNA instructions into your cells; your ribosomes read that mRNA and make a spike protein; your immune system learns to recognize it. Understanding the central dogma makes this make sense.
✓ Answer: DNA → RNA → Protein. Information flows from the master copy (DNA) to a working copy (RNA) to the functional product (protein). This applies to virtually every living thing on Earth.

✎ Worked Example — Confused about active vs. passive transport across membranes?
You keep mixing up simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and active transport on quizzes. They all move stuff across membranes. What is the difference?
1Ask one question first: Does the molecule move WITH its concentration gradient (high to low) or AGAINST it (low to high)?
2With the gradient = no energy needed (passive): Simple diffusion: small, nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂) slip directly through the lipid bilayer. Facilitated diffusion: polar or charged molecules (glucose, ions) need a protein channel or carrier, but still no ATP required.
3Against the gradient = energy required (active): Active transport uses ATP to pump molecules from low to high concentration. The classic example is the Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase pump in your neurons — it pushes 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in, against their gradients, to maintain the resting membrane potential.
4Common mistake: Students often say "facilitated diffusion uses energy because it uses a protein." It does not. Proteins are tools, not fuel. Energy cost is determined by direction relative to the gradient, not by whether a protein is involved.
✓ Answer: With gradient = passive (no ATP). Against gradient = active (needs ATP). Facilitated diffusion is passive even though it uses a protein channel.

✎ Worked Example — How do I set up and solve a dihybrid cross?
Your genetics professor gives you a cross between two pea plants that are both heterozygous for seed color (Yy) and seed shape (Rr). What fraction of offspring will have yellow, round seeds?
1Know your dominance: Yellow (Y) is dominant over green (y). Round (R) is dominant over wrinkled (r). Any genotype with at least one dominant allele shows the dominant phenotype.
2Apply Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment: Because the two genes are on different chromosomes, you can solve each gene separately and multiply. YyxYy gives 3/4 yellow (YY + Yy + Yy = 3 out of 4). RrxRr gives 3/4 round.
3Multiply the probabilities: P(yellow AND round) = 3/4 × 3/4 = 9/16. This is why the classic dihybrid ratio is 9:3:3:1.
4Common mistake: Students try to draw a 16-square Punnett square without setting it up correctly. The shortcut above (treat each gene independently, then multiply) is faster and less error-prone for dihybrids.
✓ Answer: 9/16 of offspring will have yellow, round seeds. The dihybrid ratio is 9 (dominant-dominant) : 3 (dominant-recessive) : 3 (recessive-dominant) : 1 (recessive-recessive).

✎ Worked Example — How do I write a proper lab hypothesis?
You are testing whether increasing light intensity increases the rate of photosynthesis in spinach leaves. Your professor says your hypothesis is "not scientific enough." How do you fix it?
1Use "If...then...because" format: A scientific hypothesis is a testable, falsifiable prediction with a mechanistic explanation. It is not a question, and it is not just a guess.
2Identify your variables: Independent variable (what you change): light intensity. Dependent variable (what you measure): rate of photosynthesis (measured as O₂ produced or CO₂ consumed per unit time). Controlled variables: temperature, CO₂ concentration, leaf area, water.
3Write the hypothesis: "If light intensity is increased, then the rate of photosynthesis in spinach leaves will increase, because light provides the energy that drives the light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis (exciting chlorophyll electrons), and more photons available means more reactions per unit time."
4Check falsifiability: Can this be proven wrong? Yes — if higher light does not increase O₂ production, the hypothesis is falsified. Good. That is science.
✓ Answer: If [independent variable], then [dependent variable outcome], because [mechanism]. Always include a "because" clause — that is what makes it a scientific hypothesis rather than a prediction.

✎ Worked Example — How do I calculate the molarity of a solution?
You need to prepare 500 mL of a 0.25 M NaCl solution for a biology lab. How many grams of NaCl do you need? (Molecular weight of NaCl = 58.44 g/mol)
1Recall the molarity formula: Molarity (M) = moles of solute / liters of solution. You want M = 0.25 mol/L and volume = 0.500 L.
2Find moles needed: Rearrange: moles = M × V = 0.25 mol/L × 0.500 L = 0.125 mol NaCl.
3Convert moles to grams: grams = moles × molecular weight = 0.125 mol × 58.44 g/mol = 7.31 g NaCl.
4Lab procedure: Weigh out 7.31 g NaCl, dissolve it in about 400 mL of distilled water, then add water up to the 500 mL mark on a volumetric flask. Common mistake: students add the NaCl to 500 mL of water, which gives a final volume greater than 500 mL.
✓ Answer: 7.31 g of NaCl dissolved and brought up to 500 mL total volume gives a 0.25 M NaCl solution.
✎ Worked Example — Why is organic chemistry so hard, and how do I approach it?
You just started Organic Chemistry I and feel immediately lost. There seem to be hundreds of reactions to memorize. What is the right approach?
1Stop memorizing, start understanding mechanisms: Organic chemistry is not about memorizing reactions — it is about understanding electron flow. Electrons move from nucleophiles (electron-rich, negative) to electrophiles (electron-poor, positive). Almost every reaction you will see follows this logic.
2Learn to draw curved arrows: Every mechanism in orgo is communicated with curved arrows showing where electrons move. If you can draw the arrows, you understand the reaction. Practice drawing arrows for 30 minutes a day in the first few weeks.
3Group reactions by mechanism type: Rather than memorizing 50 separate reactions, learn 5 to 6 mechanism types (addition, elimination, substitution, oxidation, reduction). Then each reaction is just an example of a type you already know.
4Use the Orgo I guide: Focus on the flashcards for functional group identification first, then the quiz for reaction type practice. The short answer section will force you to explain mechanisms in writing — exactly what exams require.
✓ Strategy: Learn electron flow logic, not individual reactions. Master curved arrows. Group reactions by mechanism type. Test yourself by explaining mechanisms out loud or in writing.

✎ Worked Example — Why do biologists need to understand logarithms?
Your professor uses log scales and pH calculations constantly. You have not thought about logarithms since high school and need a quick refresher on why they matter in biology.
1What a log means: log₁₀(x) asks "what power do I raise 10 to, to get x?" So log(100) = 2, log(1000) = 3, log(0.01) = -2. Logs convert multiplication into addition, which makes enormous ranges manageable.
2pH is a logarithm: pH = -log[H⁺]. At pH 7, [H⁺] = 10⁻⁷ M. A one-unit drop in pH (say from 7 to 6) means a 10-fold increase in H⁺ concentration. This is why a blood pH of 7.2 is a medical emergency even though it looks close to 7.4.
3Bacterial growth is exponential: One E. coli divides into 2 in 20 minutes. After 10 generations: 2¹⁰ = 1,024 bacteria. After 20 generations: 2²⁰ ≈ 1,000,000. Log-transforming growth data turns an exploding curve into a straight line, making it easy to see growth rate.
✓ Answer: Logs are essential for pH calculations, describing exponential bacterial growth, and reading semi-log graphs. Every pre-health student needs to be fluent in logarithmic thinking.

✎ Worked Example — I want to go to medical school. What GPA and MCAT do I actually need?
You are a freshman biology major who wants to become a physician. You have heard scary things about GPA cutoffs and MCAT scores. What are realistic targets and when should you start preparing?
1Understand the real numbers: For allopathic medical schools (MD), the median accepted applicant has roughly a 3.7 to 3.8 GPA and a 511 to 512 MCAT (out of 528). DO programs typically have slightly lower averages (around 3.5 GPA, 503 to 505 MCAT), but they are still highly competitive.
2GPA trajectory matters: Admissions committees look at trends. A 3.5 with a strong upward trend (struggling freshman year, excelling junior year) is often evaluated more favorably than a flat 3.6. Every semester matters — do not write off any year.
3Start MCAT prep early: Most students take the MCAT the spring of junior year. That means serious prep begins in fall of junior year at the latest. The MCAT tests content from Gen Chem, Organic Chem, Bio, Biochemistry, Physics, Psychology, and Sociology — all of which you are building now.
4GPA is necessary but not sufficient: Clinical experience, research, volunteering, shadowing, and strong letters of recommendation are all required. Use the "Finding YOUR Path" guide to build your full application timeline starting now.
✓ Target: ~3.7+ GPA and ~511+ MCAT for MD. ~3.5+ GPA and ~505+ MCAT for DO. But start building your application holistically from day one — grades alone will not get you in.
