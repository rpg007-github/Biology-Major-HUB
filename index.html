Worked Example

How to Use This Hub When You Are Overwhelmed

✎ Worked Example — I have an exam tomorrow and I am lost. Where do I start?
You have an exam on Cell Biology in 24 hours. You have fallen behind on lectures and do not know where to start.
1Triage first: Open the relevant guide (e.g., Cell Biology Part 1). Scan the table of contents. Identify the 3–4 topics your professor emphasized most. Do not try to learn everything.
2Flashcards first: Run through the flashcards for your priority topics. This builds vocabulary fast. You cannot answer process questions about terms you cannot define.
3Take the quiz: Every wrong answer tells you exactly where your gap is. Read the explanation for each wrong answer. Focus your reading time only on those gaps.
4Write one short answer: Before sleeping, write out one explanation of a key topic in your own words, without looking at notes. If you can write it, you understand it.
✓ In 2–3 hours you will have covered priority material using active recall, which is far more effective than re-reading.

✎ Worked Example — Active vs. Passive Transport
You keep mixing up simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and active transport on quizzes.
1Ask one question first: Does the molecule move WITH its concentration gradient (high → low) or AGAINST it (low → high)?
2With the gradient = passive (no ATP): Simple diffusion: small nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂) slip through the lipid bilayer. Facilitated diffusion: polar or charged molecules (glucose, ions) use a protein channel — still no ATP.
3Against the gradient = active (needs ATP): The Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase pump pushes 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in, against their gradients, to maintain the resting membrane potential.
4Common mistake: Students say "facilitated diffusion uses energy because it uses a protein." It does not. Proteins are tools, not fuel. Direction relative to gradient determines energy cost.
✓ With gradient = passive (no ATP). Against gradient = active (ATP required). Facilitated diffusion is passive even though it uses a protein.

✎ Worked Example — Dihybrid Cross
Two pea plants heterozygous for seed color (Yy) and seed shape (Rr) are crossed. What fraction of offspring have yellow, round seeds?
1Dominance: Yellow (Y) dominates green (y). Round (R) dominates wrinkled (r). Any genotype with at least one dominant allele shows the dominant phenotype.
2Treat each gene independently (Law of Independent Assortment): Yy × Yy → 3/4 yellow. Rr × Rr → 3/4 round.
3Multiply: P(yellow AND round) = 3/4 × 3/4 = 9/16. This is why the dihybrid ratio is 9:3:3:1.
✓ 9/16 of offspring will show yellow, round seeds. Shortcut: solve each gene separately, then multiply — faster and less error-prone than a 16-box Punnett square.

✎ Worked Example — Dilution Calculation (C₁V₁ = C₂V₂)
You have a 1.0 M NaCl stock solution. How do you make 200 mL of a 0.25 M solution?
1Write the dilution equation: C₁V₁ = C₂V₂ where C = concentration, V = volume.
2Plug in what you know: C₁ = 1.0 M, V₁ = unknown, C₂ = 0.25 M, V₂ = 200 mL.
3Solve for V₁: V₁ = (C₂ × V₂) / C₁ = (0.25 × 200) / 1.0 = 50 mL.
4Make the solution: Measure 50 mL of the 1.0 M stock. Transfer to a 200 mL volumetric flask. Add distilled water until the volume reaches exactly 200 mL. Mix.
✓ Take 50 mL of the 1.0 M stock and bring the total volume to 200 mL with water. This gives you 0.25 M NaCl.

✎ Worked Example — What GPA and MCAT Do I Actually Need for Medical School?
You are a freshman biology major who wants to become a physician. You have heard scary things about GPA cutoffs and MCAT scores. What are realistic targets?
1Know the real numbers: For MD programs, the median accepted applicant has roughly a 3.7–3.8 GPA and a 511–512 MCAT (out of 528). DO programs typically average around 3.5 GPA and 503–505 MCAT — still highly competitive.
2GPA trajectory matters: Admissions committees look at trends. A 3.5 with a strong upward trend is often evaluated more favorably than a flat 3.6 with no growth.
3MCAT timing: Most students take the MCAT the spring of junior year. Serious prep begins the fall of junior year at the latest. The MCAT tests Gen Chem, Organic Chem, Bio, Biochemistry, Physics, Psychology, and Sociology — all of which you are building now.
4Grades are not enough: Clinical experience, research, volunteering, shadowing, and strong letters of recommendation are all required. Start building your application holistically from day one.
✓ Target: ~3.7+ GPA and ~511+ MCAT for MD programs. ~3.5+ GPA and ~505+ for DO programs. Start building the full application from freshman year.

✎ Worked Example — I keep failing quizzes even though I re-read my notes. Why?
You spend hours re-reading notes and the textbook. Your quiz scores are not improving. You feel like you understand the material but blank on tests.
1Recognize the illusion of fluency: Re-reading creates a feeling of familiarity. Familiarity is not the same as being able to retrieve information under pressure. This is the most common study trap in college biology.
2Switch to active recall: Close your notes. Write down everything you remember about a topic on a blank page. Then check what you missed. Retrieval practice strengthens memory far more than re-reading.
3Use these guides differently: Start with the quiz or short answer — before reading the content. Struggle with the question first, then read the explanation. This "desirable difficulty" makes the learning stick.
4Space it out: Studying for 30 minutes on five different days beats studying for 2.5 hours the night before the exam. Use spaced repetition, especially for flashcards.
✓ Switch from passive re-reading to active retrieval. Students who make this change typically see significant improvement in 2–3 weeks.
